Case study · Enterprise · Enoterra

ERP System Improvement

Design consulting and platform enhancement

Enoterra — a wine distributor in Poland and Europe — needed product design to fix an ERP built without design input: unclear workflows, confusing order statuses, no home dashboard. I delivered research, interactive prototypes, and a redesign centred on Pulpit (operational dashboard), a three-step order model, and AI-assisted wine substitution.

Prototyped features

Operational Dashboard

Home screen with KPIs, trends, notifications, AI alerts and quick actions for daily tasks — orders, shipments, stock, clients, reports. Pulpit replaces sub-menu hunting; AI entry point on the dashboard.

Light & dark mode

System-wide light and dark theme — one sidebar toggle, preference saved per user. Charts, glass surfaces and status colours tuned for both modes.

AI Assistant (Enoterra AI)

Context-aware on every screen: alerts for delays, low stock and expiry; reversible change log. Wine substitutions return in-stock alternatives in the same price tier — Dodaj zamówienie opens a pre-filled order.

Command Palette (⌘K)

Single search field for navigation, quick actions and AI queries.

Order workflow & wine substitution

Three local statuses — Przygotowane, Wysłane, Anulowane — with warehouse progress in a separate column. Every change via row menu + confirmation. Out-of-stock wine: account manager describes client and quantity in AI chat; system suggests available alternatives and pre-fills the order.

Validation

Five roles from discovery tested the prototype in moderated sessions — same task scripts as the workshop baseline, plus stress cases for AI and order statuses.

Handover

Three deliverables for client handover:

  • Product showcase — key flows (dashboard, AI, ⌘K, orders) mapped to research findings.
  • Functional workshop — hands-on prototype walkthrough with the team.
  • Reference documentation — deck and operator manual for interaction patterns, edge cases and AI logic.

Two moderated rounds with the same five participants (Account Manager, Sales Manager, System Administrator, Director, Owner) and the same five task scripts. Round 1: retrospective walkthrough of the old system — step counts, timings and a post-task frustration rating. Round 2: identical scripts on the interactive prototype, with the facilitator logging clicks, screen changes, errors and warehouse workarounds live.

Five task scripts

Each participant ran every script in role. Wording was fixed; order was rotated between sessions to avoid learning bias.

  1. Morning check-in — “Start your day — what needs action first?” Scan Pulpit KPIs and notifications, then go to Zamówienia to find orders that need attention. Timed; frustration rated after.
  2. Wine substitution — “Client Win is out of Vino Prosecco Doc Treviso Extra Dry — find an alternative for 10 bottles and place the order.” Account managers only; facilitator counted navigation steps and any “call the warehouse” workaround. Step count; frustration rated after.
  3. Order status change — “Send order #2847 and cancel order #2812 — both require confirmation.” Role-play with intentional mis-clicks; logged wrong status picks, undo actions and re-edits. Error count; frustration rated after.
  4. Add product — “Receive 240 bottles of Barolo Riserva 2018 from Fontanafredda — add to the system.” Tested via AI chat with confirm card (round 2) vs. manual path discussion (round 1). Steps + time; frustration rated after.
  5. Sales snapshot — “The director asked for this month’s sales — find it in the system.” Navigate to Raporty → Sprzedaż tab, read revenue and month goal vs. target, then export PDF or request AI summary. Timed; frustration rated after.

How results were tracked

Pre-rollout only — same people who shaped requirements, so baseline numbers could be challenged in the room.

  • Steps & time — screen and clicks recorded in Lookback (navigation paths, timestamps); facilitator annotated errors and workarounds live. Stopwatch on Tasks 1, 4 and 5.
  • Order errors — wrong status picks and re-edits in Task 3; weekly baseline from workshop estimates (“how often per week”).
  • Warehouse calls — verbalised or noted whenever a participant would “call/message the warehouse”; dropped in 4 of 5 Task 2 runs on the prototype.
  • Frustration — after each task: “How frustrating was this task?” (1 = not at all · 5 = very frustrating). Mean of 5 participants × 5 tasks = 25 ratings per round → 4.2 (old flow, round 1) → 2.0 (prototype, round 2). Debrief notes tagged for confusion and workarounds.

Next steps

  • Production rollout — ship validated flows: Pulpit, order statuses, AI assistant and ⌘K.
  • Reporting — financial data views, history updates and AI-generated report summaries.
  • Warehouse — WMS integration and stock movement tracking.
  • Live metrics — track adoption and task performance in production after launch.

Simplify the workflow first, then automate

I came in thinking AI was the answer. Workshops and testing showed the opposite — warehouse calls, duplicate entry and wrong status clicks were workarounds, not mistakes. Each one became a design rule. We validated structure with the full team before adding automation. Automation works when the workflow is clear. Before that, it just speeds up the mess.
